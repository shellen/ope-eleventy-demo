---
title: Why Feeds Still Matter
date: 2026-03-15
---

The feed is one of the oldest and most resilient ideas on the web. RSS was born in 1999. Atom followed in 2003. JSON Feed arrived in 2017. Through every platform cycle, from blogs to social networks to newsletters to algorithmic timelines, the humble feed has persisted.

Why? Because feeds solve a fundamental problem: letting you choose what to read, from whom, on your own terms. No algorithm deciding what you see. No platform inserting itself between writer and reader. Just a URL that contains structured updates.

The platforms that replaced feeds didn't solve a better problem. They solved an easier onboarding problem. Subscribing to an RSS feed required knowing what a feed reader was. Following someone on Twitter required one click. But the trade-off was giving up control over your attention.

We're now seeing the pendulum swing back. Newsletter platforms like Substack and Ghost proved that people will pay for writing. But they recreated the same lock-in problem: your subscription exists only inside their ecosystem. Leave the platform, lose your subscribers.

What if subscriptions were as portable as the feeds themselves?
